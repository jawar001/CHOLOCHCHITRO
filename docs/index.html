Chapter I
1956–1969

The First Light

A nation finds its reel

On 3 August 1956, Abdul Jabbar Khan's Mukh O Mukhosh — "The Face and the Mask" — flickered to life in a Dhaka cinema. It was the first full-length feature ever made in East Bengal, hand-cranked through a borrowed camera and a borrowed hope. From that single screening, a national cinema was born. Studios rose along the canals of Tejgaon. Audiences crowded into the new Lahore-style halls. A young industry began to find a face that was its own.

  • 1956 Mukh O Mukhosh — Bangladesh's first feature film, directed by Abdul Jabbar Khan.
  • 1959 Akash Ar Mati — early experiments with rural realism.
  • 1964 Kakhono Asheni — Razzak's quiet rise begins.
  • 1966 Behula — the launching pad for "Nayak Raj" Razzak.
1970–1979
Chapter II
1970–1979

Liberation & the New Wave

A language becomes a nation

Then came 1971. Reels were smuggled across borders. Cameras turned into witnesses. Zahir Raihan's Stop Genocide screamed the war into the world's living rooms, and his Jibon Theke Neya had already coded resistance into the language of melodrama. After independence, a generation of directors — Khan Ataur Rahman, Subhash Dutta, Alamgir Kabir — rebuilt a cinema for a new country, in a new tongue, with new wounds.

  • 1970 Jibon Theke Neya — Zahir Raihan's allegory of language and freedom.
  • 1971 Stop Genocide — wartime documentary that travelled the world.
  • 1972 Ora Egaro Jon — the first post-independence Liberation War feature.
  • 1979 Surjo Dighal Bari — Sheikh Niamat Ali & Mashiuddin Shaker's poetic realism.
1980–1995
Chapter III
1980–1995

The Golden Age

Stars under the beam

For fifteen luminous years, the cinema halls of Dhaka were temples. Beder Meye Josna sold out for months. Razzak and Kabori became national myths, and Salman Shah turned mortality into matinee legend. Shabana, Bobita, Anwar Hossain, Alamgir, Ilias Kanchan — every face was a poster, every song a cassette in every rickshaw. The industry, now nicknamed "Dhallywood," released hundreds of films a year, and Bangladesh fell head over heels for itself.

  • 1989 Beder Meye Josna — record-breaking folk-fantasy hit.
  • 1993 Keyamot Theke Keyamot — Salman Shah and Moushumi's debut.
  • 1994 Aguner Poroshmoni — Humayun Ahmed's Liberation War elegy.
  • 1995 Padma Nadir Majhi — Goutam Ghose's adaptation of Manik Bandopadhyay.
1996–2005
Chapter IV
1996–2005

Transition

A new screen blinks

Salman Shah's death in 1996 punctured the dream. Cable television, VCDs and a flood of imported films cooled the cinema halls. But in the cracks, something quieter took root: the parallel cinema of Tareque Masud, Tanvir Mokammel, Morshedul Islam — film as memory, as document, as art. The commercial machine sputtered; the auteur cinema began to write its own script.

  • 1999 Chitra Nodir Pare — Tanvir Mokammel's elegy on partition and home.
  • 2002 Matir Moina — Tareque Masud at Cannes; first Bangladeshi film at the festival.
  • 2003 Lalsalu — Tanvir Mokammel adapts Syed Waliullah.
  • 2004 Shyamol Chhaya — Humayun Ahmed's Liberation War return.
2006–2015
Chapter V
2006–2015

The Renaissance

Clay birds & festival lights

A new generation arrived with cameras small enough to fit a backpack and ambitions wide as the Padma. Mostofa Sarwar Farooki re-wired the language of Bangladeshi storytelling. Amitabh Reza Chowdhury, Abu Sayeed, Nurul Alam Atique, Akram Khan turned scripts into festival contenders. Audiences came back — to multiplexes this time — and discovered that the country's cinema could be both popular and personal.

  • 2009 Third Person Singular Number — Farooki's urban rupture.
  • 2012 Television — Farooki at Busan; a screen-against-the-screen.
  • 2014 Jalal's Story — Abu Shahed Emon's mosaic of three lives.
  • 2015 Anil Bagchi'r Ekdin — Morshedul Islam's wartime portrait.
2016–2026
Chapter VI
2016–2026

The New Frontier

Stream, festival, repeat

The lens widened again. Aynabaji proved a homegrown thriller could outsell the imports. Doob went to Moscow, Hawa went viral, Saturday Afternoon went to Berlin. Streaming platforms gave debut directors a runway, and a new audience — phone in hand, subtitles on — began to ask the question every cinema must answer: what story do we tell next?

  • 2016 Aynabaji — Amitabh Reza Chowdhury's box-office reset.
  • 2017 Doob — Farooki x Irrfan Khan; Moscow premiere.
  • 2022 Hawa — Mejbaur Rahman Sumon's seafaring sensation.
  • 2023 Saturday Afternoon — Mostofa Sarwar Farooki at Berlin & Moscow.
Cast

Legendary Faces

Ten faces a country grew up watching — from Razzak's quiet kingship to Jaya Ahsan's restless reinvention.

Razzak
Nayak Raj · 1942–2017
"The King of Heroes." Over 300 films from Behula (1966) to a national-icon retirement.
Kabori
Mishti Meye · 1950–2021
The "sweet face" of Bangla cinema; debuted opposite Razzak in Sutorang (1964).
Shabana
Leading Lady · b. 1952
Nine-time National Film Award winner; matriarch of the Golden Age.
Bobita
Ashani Sanket · b. 1953
Discovered by Satyajit Ray for Ashani Sanket; Bangladeshi cinema's crossover star.
Anwar Hossain
Nawab of Bengal · 1931–2013
Towering presence as Siraj-ud-Daulah; a face the nation memorised.
Salman Shah
Eternal Romantic · 1971–1996
27 films in 4 years. Died at 25; his face still sells posters.
Manna
The Action King · 1964–2008
300+ films; redefined commercial action in the 90s and 2000s.
Shabnur
90s Sweetheart · b. 1976
Five-time National Award winner; defined romance for a generation.
Chanchal Chowdhury
Quiet Storm · b. 1974
From Television to Hawa, the actor every contemporary auteur wants.
Jaya Ahsan
Renaissance Lead · b. 1972
National Award winner across both Dhaka and Kolkata; the face of the new wave.
Direction

Visionary Directors

Ten people who held the camera against a country and pressed record — from a borrowed lens in 1956 to streaming-era auteurs.

Abdul Jabbar Khan
The First Director · 1916–1993
Made Mukh O Mukhosh (1956) — the country's first feature film.
Zahir Raihan
Wartime Witness · 1935–1972
Jibon Theke Neya, Stop Genocide. Disappeared on 30 January 1972, looking for his brother.
Khan Ataur Rahman
Composer-Director · 1928–1997
Nobab Sirajuddoula and Sujon Sokhi; a polymath of the early studio years.
Alamgir Kabir
Auteur Theorist · 1938–1989
Surjo Kanya (1976), Mohona, Rupali Soikote — taught a generation what cinema could be.
Subhash Dutta
Realist · 1930–2012
Sutorang, Aalingan, Aabirbhab — quiet realism in a melodramatic decade.
Tareque Masud
Cannes Pioneer · 1956–2011
Matir Moina (2002) — first Bangladeshi film at Cannes. Lost too soon.
Tanvir Mokammel
Memory-Keeper · b. 1955
Chitra Nodir Pare, Lalsalu — the conscience of parallel Bangladeshi cinema.
Humayun Ahmed
Storyteller · 1948–2012
Aguner Poroshmoni, Shyamol Chhaya — the most-read novelist who became a director.
Mostofa Sarwar Farooki
New-Wave Architect · b. 1973
Television, Doob, Saturday Afternoon — the festival face of Bangladesh today.
Amitabh Reza Chowdhury
Box-Office Reset · b. 1976
Aynabaji (2016) brought a multiplex generation back to Bangladeshi cinema.
